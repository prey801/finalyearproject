# Malaria Treatment Guidelines (Summary)

This document summarizes general treatment approach categories for malaria,
based on widely published WHO treatment guidance. It is intended as
reference context for an AI clinical decision-support tool, NOT as a
substitute for current national or WHO protocols, package inserts, or
clinician judgment. Specific dosing must always be confirmed against the
current official guideline and adjusted for patient weight, age, pregnancy
status, comorbidities, and local drug-resistance patterns before any
treatment decision.

## Diagnosis confirmation

Parasitological confirmation (microscopy, RDT, or PCR) is required before
starting treatment whenever feasible. Species identification matters:
*P. falciparum* and *P. knowlesi* can progress to severe disease rapidly,
while *P. vivax* and *P. ovale* require additional treatment (radical cure)
to clear liver-stage hypnozoites and prevent relapse.

## Uncomplicated malaria

First-line treatment for uncomplicated *P. falciparum* malaria is an
artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) — e.g. artemether-lumefantrine,
artesunate-amodiaquine, or dihydroartemisinin-piperaquine, depending on
local availability and resistance patterns. ACTs pair a fast-acting
artemisinin derivative with a longer-acting partner drug to clear parasites
quickly and reduce recrudescence/resistance risk.

For uncomplicated *P. vivax*, *P. ovale*, or *P. malariae*, chloroquine
remains effective in most regions without documented chloroquine resistance;
an ACT is used where resistance is present. *P. vivax* and *P. ovale* also
require a course of primaquine (or tafenoquine) for radical cure of
liver-stage hypnozoites, after screening for G6PD deficiency (primaquine
and tafenoquine can cause severe hemolysis in G6PD-deficient patients).

## Severe malaria

Severe malaria (any Plasmodium species, but especially *P. falciparum* and
*P. knowlesi* with signs such as impaired consciousness, severe anemia,
respiratory distress, jaundice, renal impairment, or high parasitemia) is a
medical emergency. First-line treatment is IV or IM artesunate, followed by
a full oral ACT course once the patient can tolerate oral medication.
Quinine is an alternative where artesunate is unavailable. Severe malaria
requires urgent referral/admission and supportive care (fluid management,
glucose monitoring, seizure management as needed).

## Special populations

- **Pregnancy**: treatment choice differs by trimester; some ACTs are
  preferred over others, and primaquine/tafenoquine are generally avoided.
  Always follow obstetric/malaria co-management protocols.
- **Children**: dosing is weight-based; several ACTs have pediatric
  formulations. Younger infants may require specialist input.
- **Drug resistance**: local resistance surveillance data should guide
  first-line ACT partner-drug selection where available.

## Parasitemia and severity

Parasitemia level is one input into severity assessment, not the sole
determinant — a patient can have severe disease at moderate parasitemia
(especially non-immune travelers) or tolerate higher parasitemia with fewer
symptoms in areas of high transmission (partial immunity). Very high
parasitemia (commonly cited threshold: >4-5% in non-immune patients, higher
in semi-immune populations) is itself a marker warranting close monitoring
and consideration of severe-malaria-level care even without other severity
signs.

## Follow-up

Patients started on treatment should be monitored for clinical response and
recurrence. Persistent fever/parasitemia after 72 hours of appropriate
treatment warrants reassessment for possible resistance, alternate
diagnosis, or complication.
